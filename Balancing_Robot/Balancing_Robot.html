Balancing Robot


This project began because I saw a video about a robot called "Ascento". Ascento is a balancing robot capable of jumping and staying stable on uneven surfaces. I 
thought this project was so cool and after a bit of pondering I was pretty confident I could build a version of my own. And not only that, but a version of my own
made mainly out of old 3D printer parts.

All the parts I initially made a concept out of I already had on hand. So, one night I fell down a CAD rabbit hole and threw together a quick design for a prototype.
The first image here shows that CAD. it was made with the idea that it would house 4 stepper motors, a power supply and a board with some electronics (And of course 
fastners and such). All from spare parts besides a few fastners and a 3D printed shell.

The electronics started on a bread board programmed on an arduino, but for size and wifi capabilities I switched to an esp32. I sat for 6 hours straight soldering the 
female header pins onto the proto-board and it works almost perfectely. The only connection issue is a short between the RX and TX pins which I am actively trying to
find and remove. As for the actual code, only connections work as of summer 2025. The actual controls are not able to keep the robot stable. Yet.
    

After putting the whole thing together and throwing some electronics on a bread board, I realized "WoW, this is so cool, If only the stepper drivers didn't burn out
in 5 minutes... oh the motors also don't have enough torque... shoot, the frame is thin as well... and it would be great if the friction fit bearing slots were tighter
...dang." Little did I know, this was gonna take a lot more iteration than I thought. But for a first prototype, it got inputs mapped to outputs and worked as a proof
of concept for me. Next protoype will keep a few things in mind. Mainly functionality wise, but another thing to consider is the asthetic of the piece. As it currently
stands, it is very hard to put away or take with me when I travel because of the odd shape. Next version will take this into account.
    

We're so back. kinda. Rev 2 is here and a lot of design considerations went into it. For this version I made it a bit more compact so its easier to carry round and
wire up. I made it so the prototype board from the last version fits within the front of the casing. I also cut out a large portyion of the empty space to be more
compact. Now the third and largest change is the knee joint housed around the top set of stepper motors. The stepper motors are supposed to be able to lift the robot
and carry most of its weight, so it needs to be high torque i.e.: the problem from the first design. To try and solve this issue I incorporated a 4:1 planetary gear
box on both of the lifting steppers and well as modifying the linkage geometry to better fit the motion I was looking for. I got the ratio and linkage lengths after
doing some calculations, but I slightly under estimated the weight each motor would be driving from a fixed position. This means they are still too weak to lift it,
but far stronger than they were. Lastly, a change not shown in the pictures, I changed the stepper motor drivers on the board from A4988 drivers to TMC 2209 drivers.
These should be able to handle the rapid changes in motor direction and longterm use without overheating.
    

Next problem: Find the short to the Rx and Tx pins stopping me from Compiling and uploading code to the ESP32. From there I can work on the balance control. After balance
control is figured out I will come back to the insufficient torque on the lifting steppers. WOO!
